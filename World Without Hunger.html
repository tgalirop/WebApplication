What is Hunger?

Hunger is the feeling that signals the body's need to be nourished. In cases where the absence of food
is prolonged the term is used to describe the lack of food. Concomitantly the word is used to describe
deprivation or poverty. Hunger begins when certain nutrients begin to disappear from the blood.
A message is then sent to the part of the brain that controls hunger, which reacts like a brake on the stomach and intestines.
As long as the blood is fed normally, this center of the brain delays the energy of the stomach and intestines.
When food is missing from the blood, the center reacts immediately and activates the stomach and intestinal system, which works more intensively.

Hunger in the World

A society cannot grow normally because of the vicious circle of poverty and hunger. To understand why this happens,
we must begin our reasoning by observing the most sensitive part of the whole, which is the children.
Poverty and insufficient food supply not only create problems for the health and development of children,
but also contribute to the reproduction of unemployment and the perpetuation of poverty. In developing countries,
many children often have to help their families make a small income that will allow them to survive so that instead of going to school,
they work in the fields, thus hindering education. But even if children go to school while hungry, they face enormous difficulties
in concentrating and assimilating knowledge. Consequently, hunger robs children of the right to acquire knowledge and skills,
that is, to be educated. Continuing our reasoning, children who do not even have the basic knowledge of reading and writing,
it is very difficult in the future to find a job and almost impossible a well-paid job, something that will help them get out of the poverty they experience.
The bottom line is that poverty and hunger lead to unemployment and vice versa, unemployment leads to hunger and poverty.
In other words, a vicious circle is created. Beyond that, if in a country there are only a small number of strong,
educated and healthy people who can work, it is very difficult for that country to build a strong economy.
Thus, when the population of a state is malnourished, productivity and wealth decrease. And the cycle continues
